Overview

Designing a calmer way to manage busy weeks.

Turning scattered tasks, chats, and calendar invites into a single, friendly view.

Many freelancers and small business owners live across three or more apps just to keep their week under control—task managers, calendar tools, and messaging apps. Important work gets buried under noisy reminders and endless lists.

BusyBee brings tasks, time blocks, and reminders into one place. Instead of yet another to-do list, it guides people to choose three daily priorities, auto-groups similar work, and protects focus time with gentle nudges.

This case study focuses on the core experience: creating a weekly plan, seeing a calm day overview, and adjusting on the fly without feeling overwhelmed.

Process

From chaos to a calm, friendly schedule.

A simple four-step loop that mirrors how people actually plan their week.

01
Listen

Discovery & patterns

I started with short interviews and async surveys to understand how freelancers currently juggle tasks and time. The main pattern: tools weren’t the problem—overloaded views and unclear priorities were.

02
Frame

Problem & flow mapping

I mapped the journey from “new project” to “planned week” and defined a key UX goal: help users pick today’s top three in under 30 seconds, starting from scattered inputs.

03
Shape

Wireframes & prototypes

I explored different ways to visualize a day—stacked cards, timeline ribbons, and compact tiles—then tested a mid-fidelity prototype to see which layout felt clearest under “busy” scenarios.

04
Polish

Visual design & microcopy

I created a warm, slightly playful visual language that still feels professional. Microcopy leans on gentle prompts instead of warnings—“Protect this focus block?” instead of “Conflict!”

Design Highlights

Little details that keep BusyBee feeling light.

Reducing cognitive load through prioritization, grouping, and soft visual cues.

Three-priority rule Smart grouping Gentle nudges
  • Each day starts with a “Pick your top 3” screen instead of a full backlog, so users anchor on what really matters before opening the rest.
  • Tasks are auto-grouped by project and type (admin, creative, calls) with soft color accents—instead of heavy labels or separate tabs.
  • When the day overfills, BusyBee doesn’t shout. It suggests trade-offs: “This looks heavy. Move one task to tomorrow?”
  • Focus blocks are rendered as wider, calmer cards with a subtle glow, visually separating deep work from quick tasks.
  • A “mode” toggle lets people flip between Day view and Energy view, which reorganizes tasks based on mental load rather than time.
  • Micro-animations are minimal and purposeful: new priorities gently slide into place instead of bouncing or popping aggressively.
Outcome

Early tests, promising signals.

Even as a concept project, feedback showed BusyBee’s calm approach resonated.

I ran quick, remote usability sessions with a small group of freelancers and agency designers. The goal wasn’t to measure exact metrics yet, but to see if BusyBee actually felt calmer than their current tools.

The strongest reactions were around the three-priority flow and the way BusyBee handled overloaded days. People described it as “firm but kind”, which is exactly the tone I was aiming for.

If taken further, the next step would be to validate retention: do people still feel this calm after three weeks of real work, not just a demo?
